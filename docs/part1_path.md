### Why the Speaker Left Corporate / Entrepreneurship (verbatim and near-verbatim)

- Pivoted away from corporate because corporate culture "felt so fake and superficial."
- "The nail in the coffin was at the last company, I asked for a promotion, was told that's not how they do things here, so I quit and started my own company."

- There is no dramatic failure to point to. The pivots were about discovering fit, not surviving disaster.

### Why Academia Fit (the four reasons, in the speaker's framing)

1. **Schedule flexibility** — was recently married, had kids; academia afforded a lot of quality time with his kids.
2. **Low stress** — generally, though not always.
3. **Relative job security** — vs. having to always hustle for the next client in entrepreneurship.
4. **Many, many interesting and constantly changing projects** — *the key thing.* Always interesting because there are always new projects, and there's not always a known way to do things even from a data science point of view. Exciting to be on the forefront of knowledge and applications of data science to new fields.

Reason 4 is the one that ties back to the speaker's lifelong "interesting" compass.

### The Speaker's Compass (verbatim)

- This has been continuously true from undergrad to today. Not a phase. The whole strategy.

## Section Overview

This opening section uses the speaker's unconventional career path as a vehicle for a single message: **your process matters more than your path, and the intersections of your interests will open doors you can't plan for.** For a Masters audience that has been told to optimize and specialize since they were teenagers, this is a deliberately countercultural opening — not "here's how to plan a career in data science" but "here's how someone who never planned one ended up with a good one anyway."

The section is biographical but not a memoir. Every story should earn its place by carrying a lesson that generalizes. Two anecdotes do most of the heavy lifting and should be paired as bookends of the same lesson:

1. **The MUD-to-startup pivot.** An obscure obsession (MUDs / LPC) turned out to be exactly the skill a Seattle startup needed at exactly the right moment, and led to building things that predated the social-network era by years.
2. **The David Baker interview.** Applied for a research tech job, got pivoted into bioinformatics on the spot, by a future Nobel laureate, because the timing of the consortium's advisory-board directive happened to align with the speaker's mixed background.

Both stories have the same structure: *unplanned intersection of interests + right place at right time = door opens that you didn't know was there.*

## Throughlines

Three threads run through this section. Plant them here for harvest later in the talk:

1. **Follow what's interesting.** "Interesting" has been the speaker's primary career compass since undergrad, and it's the same compass that points to academia today. Previews Part 4 (love of science).
2. **Pay attention to environmental fit.** Knowing you're "good at" something and knowing you "want to do it every day" are different questions. You only learn the difference by trying environments.
3. **Trust the intersections.** Biology + amateur coding got the startup job. Database consulting + a biology degree got the bioinformatics role. Generalizes directly to Part 3: in an AI era, breadth and the ability to combine domains will matter more, not less.

## Tone Notes

- **Specifics over generalities throughout.** "I learned to code by writing MUDs in LPC" lands; "I had unconventional programming experience" doesn't. Use the names: LPC, microLPC, Spinner, MUDs, David Baker. They make the story real.
- **Acknowledge lifestyle benefits of academia honestly but briefly.** Lead with the interesting-problems reason. Frame flexibility/security/stress as "things I didn't know to value at your age."
- **Don't oversell the path as charmed.** Name the friction points (didn't like engineering, didn't like corporate, didn't like running companies) so the pivots feel earned, not lucky. There's no big failure story — pivots were about discovering fit.
- **The David Baker Nobel detail will land hard with this audience in 2026.** Don't undersell it, don't overplay it. One mention is enough; let the audience do the math.
- **Compress aggressively.** Cut: technical director title, names of the two companies, the sale, "Cellular and Molecular" specifically (just say biology). Keep: LPC, Spinner, microLPC, the chat-room detail, David Baker by name, "over 60 publications," the promotion-quit story verbatim.
